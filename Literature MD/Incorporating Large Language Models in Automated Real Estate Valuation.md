Housing advertisements consist of both structured and unstructured data, whereas the regression algorithms used in AVMs require numerical or categorical inputs. Thus, previ -  ous AVMs used to estimate property values are based on housing attributes that are already available in a structured format, such as total area, build year, and number of rooms. However, housing advertisements also include listing texts that contain a consid -  erable amount of unstructured data relevant to the selling price, such as listing text and image captions. This includes narrative descriptions of dwelling standard, parking condi -  tions, or recent renovations. The recent emergence of effective and reasonable access to large language models (LLMs) enables analysis, extraction, and quantification of impor -  tant information contained within such listing texts, thus enhancing the dataset of avail -  able structured data for the AVM. We investigate whether and how AVM performance can be enhanced by incorporating LLMs for feature extraction derived from OpenAI’s GPT models. We also explore how the inclusion of information extracted with help from LLMs into an AVM can increase our understanding of the perceived value of different dwelling features and develop a GPT model-based method for extracting quantitative data   from   housing   advertisements.   Rather   than   presenting   a   broad   methodological innovation, this study provides a focused, modular example of how LLMs can be oper -  ationally integrated into an existing AVM framework. Using a narrowly defined yet high- quality dataset of Oslo housing transactions over a short time period, we demonstrate a replicable and transparent pipeline that tests the practical feasibility of LLM-enhanced valuation models.  Most AVM studies have focused on integrating various machine learning (ML) methods to enhance predictability and to compare these methods with traditional residential property valuation approaches. 3   However, AVMs have also been used for the valuation of curb appeal and architectural styles (see, e.g., Johnson et al.,   2020 ; Lindenthal & Johnson,   2021 ; Wan & Lindenthal,   2023 ), assessment of the property’s technical condition on its price (Oust et al.,   2023 ), evaluation of a property’s interior (Despotovic et al.,  2023 ), and feature extraction from floor plan image segmentation (Kringeland et al.,  2025 ).  The use of textual information to construct variables for real estate valuation is not new. For instance, Haag et al. ( 2000 ) employed keywords from property listings in a hedonic pricing model. Building on this, Nowak and Smith ( 2017 ) incorporated textual data into a hedonic framework and reported a reduction in pricing errors of more than 25%. The NLP technology has significantly evolved since it was first introduced, 4   with mod -  ern LLMs such as BERT and GPT demonstrating unique and powerful abilities for both text understanding and generation. Prompt engineering—formulating and designing the prompt in a way that will result in the desired output—has proven important for getting precise and high-quality responses from GPT models (Liu et al.,   2023 ). The prompt guides the model more effectively and is a good alternative for fine-tuning models by unlocking the potential in GPT models for a certain task (OpenAI,   2024 ).  In our empirical analysis, we use a dataset containing 9,842 individual transactions of residential housing as well as advertisements on dwellings sold between January 2022   and   March   2023   in   Oslo,   Norway.   First,   we   develop   a   pipeline   that   accesses OpenAI’s GPT models, takes housing advertisements as input, and returns quantitative values.   Then,   we   perform   prompt   engineering   based   on   the   available   literature   to obtain quality data from the LLM and collect data from the pipeline. The LLM extracts features about parking, kitchen age, bathroom age, fireplaces, storage units, storage unit size, outdoor area, outdoor area size, and housing standard. After validating the effectiveness and reliability of the LLM, we develop an AVM using both the structured data from the advertisements and the unstructured data from the LLM. Because gradi -  ent   boosting   machines   have   shown   high   accuracy   in   predicting   real   estate   prices (see Birkeland et al.,   2021 ; Chen et al.,   2023 ; Helgaker et al.,   2023 ; Hjort et al.,   2022 ; Kok et al.,   2017 ; Kumkar et al.,   2018 ; Pollestad et al.,   2024 ), we use XGBoost as our preferred AVM. Finally, we validate the effectiveness and reliability of the AVM.  We find that LLMs can be used to improve AVM performance. Compared to the model without the features extracted by the LLM, the XGBoost valuation model root mean squared error (RMSE) was reduced from 10.53 to 7.97% and the mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) was reduced from 6.36 to 5.39%. The features with the most positive impact were housing standard, fireplace, and parking. Our findings can be used to improve the AVMs utilized by tax authorities, banks, real estate agents, and algorithmic traders, as well as for enhancing AVMs used for research purposes. Norwegian Housing Market  In the Norwegian market, the most common method of selling residential property is through an open English auction. Over 98% of all residential transactions are conducted through a real estate agent (Flått et al.,   2022 ). The seller employs a real estate agent who acts as both an independent third party and an auctioneer (Sønstebø et al.,   2021 ). This characteristic, coupled with the fact that nonprofessional buyers and sellers domin -  ate the Norwegian housing market, makes this context highly suitable for studies on property pricing in general (Oust et al.,   2020 ).  Our data relate to the property sales in Oslo, the capital of Norway, which is divided into 17 districts. As of December 2023, the city had around 720,000 inhabitants. Over the past few decades, the city has experienced significant and steady growth; approximately half of Norway’s population growth during this period has occurred in Oslo. Historically, the eastern part of Oslo has had generally smaller and cheaper dwellings compared to the western part (Birkeland et al.,   2021 ).  2.2. Data Description  The dataset used in this study contains 9,842 residential housing advertisements from finn.no, the main platform for listing real estate for sale in Norway. The included dwell -  ings were sold between January 2022 and March 2023 in Oslo, Norway. The dataset was provided by Solgt.no, a Norwegian Proptech company, and consists of traditional struc -  tured data about the dwellings sold as well as the advertisement text. The traditional structured dataset is comprised of 25 features, including address, sale date, and sale price. It also contains quantitative data, including size, number of floors, rooms, bedrooms, bathrooms, and the year in which the building was constructed. Additionally, the dataset includes information on ownership type, housing type, and geo -  graphical features like district, longitude, and latitude. The dataset with the advertise -  ment text contains all textual information from the housing advertisements, i.e., title, description, and image captions, which we denote as “listing text.” The listing text data is composed of clean and complete sentences from the advertisements, with unneces -  sary whitespaces removed. Testing Process for the LLM:   The effectiveness and reliability of the LLM was validated by conducting several tests and comparing the results to manually labeled data extracted from 200 housing advertisements based on the same rules as those described in the model behavior for the LLM. In total, 15 tests were conducted with the same model behavior and the same 200 housing advertisements. The tests were grouped into three sets, with five tests per set. This grouping allowed for direct comparison of the results both with the manually labeled data and among the different test groups. Each test involved a full run of the model based on the same set of 200 housing advertisements. For every run, the model generated predictions based on the rules described in the LLM’s behavior. The overall goal of the tests was to evaluate how accurately the model could replicate the manually labeled values, as these served as the “correct” reference, providing insight into its overall perform -  ance and reliability. To evaluate performance, several validation methods were used, including precision-recall analysis, correlation analysis, and percentage-based accuracy against the manual dataset. Since the test conditions remained unchanged, any variations in results were attributed to natural fluctuations in the model’s responses rather than differ -  ences in the test setup. This approach allowed for a thorough assessment of the model’s stability and performance across multiple iterations.  3.3. 5. Concluding Remarks  In this article, we explored the potential for enhancing automated valuation models (AVMs) by incorporating features extracted using large language models (LLMs). Our aim was to develop a pipeline to extract quantitative features from housing advertisements with GPT models. By utilizing a dataset containing 9,842 residential housing transactions from Oslo, and we constructed nine new features (housing standard, type of parking, kit -  chen age, bathroom age, storage size, number of storage units, type of outdoor area, size of outdoor area, and number of fireplaces) to explain property prices based on the listing text using LLM. These nine LLM features were analyzed by running a horse race among four differently specified AVMs to predict property prices. Therefore, we devel -  oped two linear (OLS) and two nonlinear (XGBoost) models, and distinguish for each of the two categories those with and without LLM features.  For the nonlinear XGBoost model, the RMSE declined from 10.53 to 7.97%, and the MAPE reduced from 6.36 to 5.39% after including the LLM features, representing an improvement of 24.31 and 15.25%, respectively. Such a strong improvement on already strong results suggest that the LLM variables contain valuable information for property valuation. If the sole purpose is to improve model performance, one can, as in Oust et al. ( 2020 ) and Birkeland et al. ( 2021 ), use information from previous transaction prices to enhance AVM estimates for property characteristics that do not change over time, but only for properties where past sales data are available.  To expand this objective and to investigate the LLM features further, their performances were plotted as SHAP dependence plots and were compared to the actual housing prices. This technique allowed us to study the price relationships between the LLM features and housing prices in detail. The price relationships from the LLM features aligned with the expectations. For example, better housing standard and having a fireplace showed a clear positive relationship with price, while the age of bathrooms and kitchens had a negative price impact. However, the method is even more detailed, as it allowed us to showcase nonlinear relationships between the variables as well as to compare, for example, different types of parking and different types of outdoor areas against each other.  The findings indicate that LLMs can effectively extract valuable information from the textual components of housing advertisements, which, when combined with traditional data, provide a more nuanced and accurate valuation model. This approach not only improves AVM accuracy but also has broader implications for real estate research, offer -  ing new avenues for exploring market dynamics that were previously difficult to quantify due to the lack of structured data. Overall, this research contributes to the growing body of literature on the ML application in real estate, particularly in the integration of LLMs for feature extraction. The results reported in this article underscore the value of combin -  ing structured and unstructured data to achieve more accurate and reliable property val -  uations,   highlighting   the   promising   future   of   AVMs   in   both   practical   and   research contexts. Rather than introducing a broad methodological innovation, this study provides a focused and modular demonstration of how LLMs can be operationally integrated into an existing AVM pipeline.  The practical implications of this research are far-reaching. Enhanced AVMs can be adopted by tax authorities, banks, real estate agents, and algorithmic traders to perform more precise property valuations. From a research perspective, our study also provides a  20   H. FAXVAAG ET AL.
