|   7  COMMERCIAL REAL ESTATE MARKETS AFTER THE END OF “LOW FOR LONG”: RISKS AND POLICY CHALLENGES © OECD 2024  Restricted Use   -   À usage restreint  Overview  Real estate assets are the largest store of wealth in the OECD countries, valued at USD 111 trillion in 2022, nearly twice the level of GDP. Commercial buildings, plus other corporate structures, at an estimated USD 38 trillion, account for more than one-third of total property assets, with the remainder residential real estate. After significant increases, commercial property prices have recently declined, particularly in the United States and the euro area, driven by both higher global interest rates and the adjustment to new demand patterns induced by growing teleworking and e-commerce. Since 2022, the financing costs of property developers and risk premia for low-rated commercial mortgage-backed securities have risen. The performance of commercial real estate investment trusts and funds has also weakened, with significant outflows from large US-based funds.  In 2023, the commercial real estate exposures of financial intermediaries are estimated to have totalled USD 21 trillion. Although loans collateralized by commercial property assets and loans to property developers account for a sizeable proportion of total bank lending, particularly in advanced economies, commercial real estate financing is largely provided by non-bank financial intermediaries. Prior to the Global Financial Crisis (GFC), commercial real estate financing was primarily from commercial mortgage- backed securities and direct loans from, and securities issuance to, banks and institutional investors. Over the past fifteen years, commercial equity and mortgage real estate investment trusts that either invest directly in property or hold property loans have significantly expanded, particularly in advanced economies and in the retail, office and industrial property sectors. Debt accumulation by commercial real estate investment trusts and property developers is reflected in a substantial increase in outstanding bonds and syndicated loans, and a growing reliance on private capital.  Cross-country information on the different aspects of commercial property financing is sparse. This paper provides an overview of commercial real estate financing and exposures by aggregating available data on different market-based instruments. It highlights a number of key challenges, risks, and policy implications from the ongoing downturn in the sector. Key financing-related issues include:  •   Commercial property developers face mounting refinancing and credit quality challenges. Over the next five years, USD 1.3 trillion in bonds and syndicated loans will mature, representing 40% of the amount outstanding in 2023. Most redemptions will be from commercial property developers, mainly in China and non-US advanced economies.  •   Debt sustainability challenges may increase for leveraged issuers, with the amount of low-credit- quality debt of the riskiest property developers now surpassing GFC levels. Mortgage credit quality is also deteriorating, as reflected by rising delinquency rates on commercial mortgage-backed securities and number of missed mortgage payments from borrowers with commercial real estate loans.  •   Deteriorating credit quality in the commercial real estate sector could pose significant risks to financial stability, due to the large exposures of various financial intermediaries to the sector. Banks’ non-performing loans and potential losses for non-bank financial intermediaries could rise due to increasing financing costs and pressure on the earnings of commercial property developers. Significant   losses   at   commercial   real   estate   investment   trusts   could   spread   to   real   estate investment funds, raising investor redemptions and potentially downward price spirals from fire sales that spill over into the wider financial sector. Insurance companies’ solvency ratios and pension funds’ liquidity may also be adversely affected by rising losses, both directly from their commercial real estate loans and indirectly through their interconnectedness with real estate funds.

8   |  COMMERCIAL REAL ESTATE MARKETS AFTER THE END OF “LOW FOR LONG”: RISKS AND POLICY CHALLENGES © OECD 2024  Restricted Use   -   À usage restreint  Policy challenges remain in assessing and mitigating risks from commercial real estate exposures. Significant data gaps hinder accurate analysis of financial stability risks from those exposures across financial intermediaries. Moreover, existing macroprudential tools in commercial real estate finance are primarily bank-based, with limited measures available to monitor non-bank financial intermediaries. The use of these tools can also vary across jurisdictions. There are a number of policy options that would help in monitoring and mitigating financial stability risks from the commercial real estate sector. These include:  •   The monitoring of financial stability risks would be facilitated by cross-country agreement on a precise definition of commercial property assets and the development of common qualitative and quantitative indicators.  •   Increased evaluation of the credit quality and loss-provisioning of commercial real estate portfolios held by both banks and non-bank financial intermediaries is needed, along with enhanced banking sector stress-testing by supervisors (with account taken of indirect exposures to commercial real estate through bank funding of exposed non-bank financial intermediaries). These measures would help to identify the major vulnerabilities of interconnected providers of commercial real estate credit that   could   pose   systemic   risk   concerns,   and   would   provide   a   stronger   basis   for   policy recommendations.  •   Improved assessment and mitigation of liquidity mismatches and leverage risks for institutional investors and real estate investment funds, including by constraining redemptions in the latter, would help to strengthen financial resilience.  •   Further work on borrower-based measures (especially for non-bank financial intermediaries) and enhanced international cooperation would help to   limit excessive risk-taking and regulatory fragmentation by encouraging a broad approach to risk monitoring and supervision, and facilitating the implementation of activity-based regulatory measures.

Real estate non-bank financial intermediaries and market-based credit products  Real estate non-bank financial intermediaries  Real estate investment trusts  Within the real estate investment trust (REIT) industry, there are two broad types of trusts: equity REITs  and mortgage REITs (mREITs):  •   Equity REITs invest in physical properties and manage income-producing real estate that  typically generates earnings through rents. Most trusts specialise in a single property type,  although some manage portfolios that include multiple types of properties. Commercial equity  REITs specialise in commercial real estate, and residential equity REITs in residential real  estate.  •   mREITs invest in mortgages and mortgage-backed securities (MBS), making them real estate  debt owners. Commercial mREITs typically focus on commercial mortgages and commercial  MBS, while residential mREITs invest in residential mortgages and residential MBS. mREITs  engage in a wide range of activities related to originating, funding, servicing, restructuring and  securitising loans secured by commercial real estate. Some mREITs specialise in one or two  of these activities, while others perform several of them. Some mREITs also hold physical  residential or commercial properties as investments. mREITs earn income from the interest and  capital gains on their investments.  REITs use leverage. Equity REITs initially use the cash raised from investors to purchase tangible  properties. Similarly, mREITs use the cash they raised from investors to purchase mortgage-backed  securities. Then, REITs use tangible properties or mortgage-backed securities as collateral to borrow  money to purchase more real estate assets, a process that they repeat multiple times. However, the  number of rounds is limited as the (repo) lender requires a collateral margin on each loan, a gap that  serves as a buffer for the lender’s protection, or may impose covenants with regard to leverage. REITs  also rely on unsecured revolving credit facilities and short-term credit to manage temporary liquidity  needs.  Real estate investment funds  A real estate investment fund is a type of mutual fund that pools money from multiple investors to  primarily invest in securities offered by real estate companies, including mortgage-backed securities,  bonds and REITs shares.  While REITs pay out regular dividends or coupons, real estate investment funds reward investors  through gains in asset values. Since most trusts are listed and traded on major stock exchanges, they  tend to be more liquid than mutual investment fund shares, which can only be redeemed at the end of  the trading day when the net asset value is settled.  The option-adjusted spread helps investors compare cash flows from a fixed-income security to reference rates while also valuing embedded options against general market volatility.

Bonds and syndicated loans  While most bonds issued by property developers are standard ones, sustainable bonds form a specific  category that includes green, social, sustainability and sustainability-linked bonds. Green bonds enable  capital-raising and investment for new or existing real estate projects with environmental benefits,  including those aimed at fostering a net-zero emissions economy. Social bonds raise funds for new and  existing socially sound and sustainable real estate projects (such as social housing). Sustainability  bonds finance or re-finance a combination of both green and social real estate projects.  A syndicated loan is a commercial loan provided by a group of lenders to a property developer, initially  structured and arranged by one or more commercial or investment banks, and then syndicated to other  banks or institutional investors. Debt sustainability challenges in leveraged commercial real estate finance would be exacerbated if tight financial conditions were to prompt institutional investors to reduce the risk of their portfolios. During the prolonged period of low interest rates following the GFC, institutional investors favoured high-yielding bonds   and   direct   loans   (including   to   property   developers)   to   enhance   their   ability   to   fulfil   future commitments. Tighter monetary policy has made bonds, particularly investment-grade rated corporate and government conventional bonds, more attractive to institutional investors. The performance of commercial property investments has also been eroded by declining property prices. A swift change in institutional investors’ investment strategies would heighten funding challenges for leveraged commercial real estate issuers, and potentially raise delinquencies. Heterogeneous implementation of macroprudential policy tools in commercial real estate finance across jurisdictions  The use of macroprudential policy tools to mitigate commercial property risks is more challenging than in residential real estate markets due to the complexity and heterogeneity of the commercial property sector, the range of market participants and instruments, and data quality issues related to lending standards for commercial real estate loans (ECB, 2022 [22]   ; ESRB, 2023 [19]   ). Each commercial real estate sub-sector and market segment has distinct characteristics and interconnections with the broader economy. Assessments of collateral value may also be challenging as historical valuation data may not always be readily accessible or still relevant at the time of loan issuance. This is particularly the case in market segments with limited transactions. Verifying a borrower’s own resources can also prove challenging due to complex ownership structures and potential reliance on revolving market financing.  The macroprudential toolkit in real estate finance remains primarily bank-based ( Box 2 ). The main targeted policy tools include ex-post capital-based and ex-ante borrower-based measures. Capital-based measures are intended to boost lender resilience – by adding macroprudential capital requirements (e.g. higher risk weights or stricter criteria on risk weights, systemic sectoral capital buffers, stress-testing exercises) to cover   systemic   risks   not   reflected   in   microprudential   capital   requirements 22   –   and   support   credit intermediation following a shock. Borrower based measures are intended to limit borrowers’ excessive indebtedness and ensure solid credit quality and adequate repayment capacities, thereby limiting future non-performing loans and losses (e.g. commercial real estate specific loan-to-value ratio and debt service- to-income ratio). Financial stability would be enhanced if a level playing field was ensured by applying such activity-based tools to all providers of commercial real estate credit that could pose systemic risk concerns, whether banks or not. As all players of systemic relevance are likely to receive government support during times of distress, they should be subject to regulatory oversight to mitigate moral hazard.  22   Microprudential capital measures for banks typically include restrictions related to loan-to-valuation (LTV) ratios. The LTV ratio is the main risk driver for risk weighting purposes with a distinction between exposures secured by real estate where repayment is or is not materially dependent on rent/sale of the property. Banks are required to consider the quality of the collateral (e.g. adequate valuation, finished property), the collateral’s effectiveness (e.g. as legal enforceability, seniority of lien), and other procedural aspects (e.g. required documentation) before assigning the minimum risk weights (BIS, 2017   [72]   ). The risk weights applied for commercial real estate exposures where repayment is materially dependent on cash flows generated by property are the minimum between 60% and the risk-weight of the counterparty for an LTV lower than 60%, and otherwise the risk weight of the counterparty. The risk weight of the counterparty is set at 75% for exposures to individuals, and 85% for exposures to SMEs and other counterparties. The risk weights applied for commercial real estate exposures where repayment is not materially dependent on cash flows generated by property are 70% for an LTV below 60%, 90% for an LTV between 60% and 80%, and 110% for an LTV greater than 80%.

Credit risk exposure from commercial real estate loans for both banks and non-bank financial intermediaries could be mitigated by in-depth assessment and monitoring of the credit quality of commercial real estate portfolios and the adequacy of provisioning, with a particular focus on loans that could be negatively affected by changes in the commercial real estate sectors. Credit providers should already regularly reassess commercial real estate collateral values and the debt-servicing capacity of their borrowers, using prudent assumptions about sectoral developments, and the evolution of rents, vacancy rates and construction costs (ECB, 2024   [38]   ). However more could be done to enforce this. Supervisors should also conduct stress-testing scenarios of large commercial property price declines for all banks with material commercial real estate exposures rather than a subset of the largest banks only, with account taken of indirect exposures to commercial real estate through bank funding of exposed non- bank financial intermediaries.  In the insurance and pension fund sectors, accurate risk assessment of commercial real estate exposures and the potential liquidity mismatches between their funding and their commercial real estate investments is a prerequisite for implementing effective and efficient policy measures. In particular, understanding how collateral for commercial real estate loans and investments is treated, and how risks from commercial real estate exposures are accounted for, including indirect risks from investments in specialist investment funds, would help to improve capital requirements, investment rules and risk management practices.  Measures to address liquidity and leverage risks at open-ended real estate investment funds would help to strengthen their resilience to margin calls and redemptions (FSB, 2023 [39]   ; ECB, 2024   [40]   ). Consistent with policy recommendations from the Financial Stability Board and the International Organization of Securities Commissions (FSB, 2023 [41]   ; IOSCO, 2024 [42]   ), real estate funds should redeem shares less frequently, requiring a better alignment between fund redemption terms and the liquidity of underlying commercial real estate assets. Close monitoring of the risks arising from liquidity mismatch and leverage is also a prerequisite for the effective use of liquidity management tools and leverage limits where necessary.  Further exploratory and conceptual work on borrower-based measures for commercial real estate loans needs to reflect the heterogeneity of the commercial property sector and its financing. This includes how to use multiple tools optimally and quantify the costs and benefits precisely (BIS, 2023   [43]   ). Transparency about the challenges of using macroprudential policies to mitigate risks from commercial real estate exposures will in the long run enhance policymaking even if in the short term it might heighten scrutiny.  To effectively complement entity-based regulatory measures (such as regulations on banks), activity- based measures for commercial real estate lending should be applicable to all credit providers that are interconnected and may pose systemic risk concerns (including for cross-border lending). This idea corresponds with the “congruent principle for financial regulation” put forward by (Metrick and Tarullo, 2021 [44]   ), which states that forms of financial intermediation posing similar risks to financial stability should be regulated with similar stringency, regardless of legal form, chartering identity, or business model. Thus, the amount of systemic risk tolerated in non-bank intermediation should be similar to that implicitly incorporated in prudential regulation of banks, and reflect the same balance between short-term growth and medium-term financial stability. A holistic approach to financial sector risk monitoring and supervision (Acharya, Cetorelli and Tuckman, 2024   [45]   ), recognising the implications of complex relationships between banks, real estate investment trusts and funds, and institutional investors, would help mitigate regulatory arbitrage and negative externalities. This would prevent disruptions in credit provision or defaults that could destabilise banks, other intermediaries, and parts of the real economy.  Enhanced international cooperation is also needed to identify any additional relevant policy instruments and reduce regulatory fragmentation across jurisdictions, thereby enhancing global financial stability.

Methodology for commercial real estate market-based credit data collection and calculations  As commercial real estate investment statistics suffer from both data quality (scarce and incomplete data) and operative issues (lack of comparability across countries and – even where comparable data are available – insufficiently long time series), it is difficult to obtain a comprehensive assessment of vulnerabilities in the sector across jurisdictions globally.  The new datasets assembled for this paper are intended to provide global coverage for various credit market products, real estate investment trusts (REITs), and commercial property developers. Granular historical market and financial statement information at the firm or deal level provides a clear view of business-oriented property development and services, excludes income-generating residential real estate (such as US multi-family units), and aligns with the common definition of commercial real estate in national accounts.  Balance sheet information for listed REITs and property developers  Listed commercial equity and mortgage REITs  The unbalanced panel dataset used in this paper contains financial statement information for 801 listed commercial and residential equity REITs in 21 countries between 2000 and 2023. As such, numerous studies suggest that the level of the interest coverage ratio can be an important indicator of financial distress under high-interest rates. The criteria for decomposing debt by credit quality are based on previous OECD work (Roulet, 2020   [46]   )  Corporates with “sound credit risk profile” have a positive leverage ratio lower than 5 and an interest coverage ratio higher than 2. Leveraged corporates are companies with a leverage ratio higher than 5 or with a negative leverage ratio (due to negative EBITDA).
