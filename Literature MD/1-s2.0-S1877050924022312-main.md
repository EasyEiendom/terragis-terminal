For the respondents familiar with the framework, the complexity of real options is the main hinder for implementation [1]. Other studies confirm the small percentage of managers using this concept. For example, among Fortune's 1,000 largest companies, out of 279 respondents, 40 used real options in 2007 (14.3%) [2]. Despite the methodological difficulties described below, real options are sometimes used in various spheres of the economy, e.g. in the energy sector [3], especially renewable [4], [5] and decision making process [6], R&D research (most appropriate in managing technical risk, market risk and technology deployment) and others [7]. Real options are predominantly utilized in business areas with high uncertainty, where management, consciously or unconsciously, regularly creates and exercises these options. In R&D, real options are evident as management retains the right, but not the obligation, to commercialize outcomes. They are relevant for decisions made under conditions of risk and uncertainty, particularly those involving multiple risks and numerous configurations. Real options are also pertinent in scenarios with cascading risks and their associated decisions. The need for methods determining the value of real options and decision flexibility is therefore obvious. What hinders them are methodological issues, in particular complicated valuation algorithms, often based on financial option valuation methods that do not reflect the unique specificity of the investment. Methodological problems translate into relatively little practical application. This happens in particular due to the assumptions that are difficult to meet and the complicated estimation of the input parameters of the valuation model. Scientists are therefore looking for new, simpler methods of valuing real options, e.g. the Datar-Mathews method described below or other methods based on simulation and decision trees [8], [9]. All these attempts are intended to simplify the theory and make it easier to apply. One of the attempts to facilitate the use of real options in practice is the SCV method proposed by the author and described in the methodological part. The estimation of real options value commonly employs techniques used in financial options valuation. The authors of the most popular valuation model were Nobel laureates Fischer Black and Myron Scholes [10]. It was they who first noticed that a risk-free portfolio (arbitrage portfolio) can be constructed from stocks and options. Since arbitrage ensures that the return on a risk-free portfolio must be equal to the risk-free rate of return, thus, in combination with appropriate boundary conditions, this allowed to derive an analytical model for the valuation of European call options as a function of the stock price, the exercise price, the time to option expiration, the risk-free interest rate, and the variance of stock price. In order for the model to operate efficiently in the financial market, it had to adopt numerous assumptions aimed at creating conditions for a perfect market. It was assumed that: the risk-free rate remains constant throughout the option's expiration period, stock returns over infinitely small time intervals follow a normal distribution, the distribution of stock prices is lognormal, the stock does not pay dividends, short selling is possible, and the movement of stock prices can be modeled by a continuous stochastic process, such as the Ito process. These difficult to meet assumptions (as well as many others not mentioned here) made it possible to determine the analytical formula for the valuation of European call and put options. The Black-Scholes model has had a significant impact on the theory of derivative instrument valuation, market behaviors, and has contributed to the dynamic development of options markets. The Black-Scholes model is a good solution for European call and put options, but its usefulness for pricing more complex, real options is limited. Real options are often American options, they appear as a combination of several simple options and depend on several sources of risk that change over time. Each complicated case of real options would have to have a separate analytical solution, which could be problematic. An exemplary modification of the Black-Scholes formula is its generalization for stocks paying dividends over the life of the option, developed by Robert Merton [11]. An additional parameter y determines the value of the dividend paid or other cash flows in relation to the value of the underlying instrument. In the context of real options, the y parameter is interpreted in various ways: 1) as a reduction in the value of the investment project through the payment of dividend, 2) costs of maintaining the option - license fees, lease costs, 3) the impact of market competition. Despite the difficulties in estimating this parameter, it allows for a simplified consideration of additional factors affecting the value of the option. Analytical methods of valuing real options, i.e. the Black-Scholes model and its extensions (e.g. the above-mentioned Black-Scholes-Merton model), are problematic to apply in practice due to their rigid assumptions. Estimating the value of input parameters (e.g. volatility) to the valuation model is detached from market realities and not very intuitive for managers [12]. Analysts deal with these problems partially by using methods based on the binomial tree, which is a more flexible tool, to value real options. The concept developed by J. Cox, S. Ross and M. Rubinstein [13], as an alternative method to the Black-Scholes formula is an approximation of solutions to continuous-time models [14]. The essence of this method is to build a tree of the values of the underlying instrument, determining the boundary conditions, which leads

There are two main options valuation methods in the binomial tree - the option equivalent method and the risk indifference method [15]. The option value is calculated at the last nodes of the binomial tree, based on boundary conditions and using backward induction. The use of decision trees enables the valuation of complex, multi-stage options. It also allows you to sum up the economic effects of several different options or apply more complex conditions for the exercise of real options. However, it does not solve practical problems related to estimating the value of the underlying asset and its volatility, or taking into account many risk factors. These remain out of reach for many managers, making the use of real options theory in practice quite rare. An attempt to solve the problems related to the use of the Black-Scholes model and binomial trees is to use fuzzy sets [16] and Monte Carlo simulations for real options valuation. In most cases, the use of Monte Carlo simulations for the valuation of real options involves reproducing price changes of the underlying asset (geometric Brownian motions with a logarithmic-normal distribution) using a stochastic process [17]. The option value is determined based on the average obtained by comparing the value of the underlying instrument and the boundary conditions for each scenario [18]. Another interesting technique for valuing real options, based on Monte Carlo simulation and at the same time consistent with the Black-Scholes model [19], is the Datar-Mathews method [20]. It involves generating a sufficiently large number of scenarios of future operating profits (EBIT,   S   T ) and costs related to the implementation of the investment. The level of operating profit and investment outlays is treated as a random variable with a specific distribution. Costs are brought to present value using the risk-free rate (r), while operating profits are discounted in each period (t) at a cost of capital reflecting their risk (μ). Then, in each iteration, the value of investment outlays   X T  –   investment expenditures, discounted at the risk-free rate   r ) is subtracted from the operating profit, creating a distribution of the value of benefits resulting from the investment. Real option is the expected value (mean, E) from this distribution at time T = 0. This method is highly intuitive and easy to implement in a financial model using Monte Carlo simulation. Simulation comparative valuation  The Simulation comparative valuation (SCV) method is an extension of the concept of real options valuation called double   Monte Carlo (2MC) simulation, formulated by Wiśniewski in 2008 [ 21]. 2MC used Monte Carlo simulation to determine the economic result related to the use (or not) of the flexibility contained in investment projects. This method treats the cash flows of an investment project as the underlying asset, so it facilitates consolidation by aggregating all risk factors based on the MAD method introduced by Copeland and Antikarov [22]. SCV is using commonly known methodological apparatus for assessing investment performance, such as net present value (NPV). Therefore, it is necessary that the SCV approach is consistent with the basic assumptions of the NPV method. Using decision tree analysis and simulation techniques, it becomes possible to determine the value of a real option by extending the analysis of the NPV financial model. SCV is a method of valuing real options that involves a conditional comparison of the expected value of the project in which the real option is included and can be exercised (NPV   EXT   ) and the value of the investment on which the option is built (NPV   BASE   ) according to the following formula:  𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅 = 𝐸𝐸[𝑀𝑀𝑀𝑀 𝑋𝑋 (𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑁 𝑅𝑅 𝐸𝐸𝐸𝐸 𝑇𝑇   − 𝑁𝑁𝑁𝑁 𝑅𝑅 𝐵𝐵𝐵𝐵𝐵𝐵 𝐸𝐸   ; 0)]   (2) In the SCV method, the key to determining the value of a real option is to create a simulation financial model of the underlying investment (NPV   BASE   ). A base investment is defined as an investment project that does not take into

The financial model characterizes the planned investment project by describing the accompanying assumptions e.g. time horizon, cost of capital and basic data on revenues and operating costs and taking into account these variables, their interconnections (in the form of correlation and autocorrelation) and mechanisms influencing the values. Taking into account the most important risk-bearing variables for the value of the project, such as the volume of demand, production, sales, level of prices and costs, CAPEX, and their stochastic description allows in the simulation process to integrate all of them into one, easy-to-interpret distribution, which facilitates the assessment of the value and risk of the project. The value of the extended project (NPV   EXT   ), unlike that obtained from the base investment (NPV   BASE   ), takes into account all expenditure necessary to maintain and execute the real option, as well as the expected benefits accompanying its existence. The extended financial model enables easy calculation of cash flows related to the exercise of options. It takes into account all direct effects (e.g. increased sales revenues, reduction of production costs, etc.) as well as indirect effects, e.g. tax benefits and synergies. The use of financial modelling allows to precisely determine the difference between the base project and the extended project in which the option is included. Determining the value of a real option in the SCV method involves calculating the expected value from the set of differences between the value of the extended and the base project, maintaining the condition that the value of the option in each iteration must be greater than or equal to zero. The SCV algorithm is so universal that it can be used to determine the value of any real option, as well as complex options. Nevertheless, the basic and extended models should be individually constructed, adapting their structure and functions to the operational activities and characteristics of the options. There is no need to distinguish between types of options because each type of real option can be individually and precisely reproduced in the financial model. Financial modeling in the SCV method allows for determining the optimal timing of option exercise based on various criteria, and it is particularly useful for complex, multi-stage projects with multiple sources of risk. SCV is flexible and can handle interdependent, sequential, or mutually exclusive options. The method assumes valuation in an incomplete market, where risk is reflected in cash flows and the cost of capital. It enables a comprehensive risk analysis by incorporating stochastic input variables and their interrelationships. The value of the option is determined by comparing simulated financial models that represent scenarios with and without the real option. SCV can also provide additional insights, such as the distribution of the base model and the decision variable, as well as the probability of exercising the option. The SCV method can be practically applied in various investment scenarios characterized by high uncertainty and multiple sources of risk, for example:  •   Development of new technologies, to evaluate investment options, where decisions on project continuation are based on the outcomes of initial research and development stages.  •   Exploration of natural resources, to assess options related to exploring new deposits of natural resources, where further investment decisions depend on the results of initial drilling and geological surveys.  •   Infrastructure expansion, to evaluate options related to expanding existing infrastructure, such as building new facilities or modernizing old ones, where subsequent project stages are carried out based on preliminary results and market analyses.  •   Market entry, where decisions on further investments are made based on initial market research and pilot operation outcomes.  •   Product development, to evaluate options related to launching new products, where decisions on continuing or halting production depend on market test results and demand analyses.  •   Mergers and acquisitions, to analyse options related to strategic decisions, where subsequent steps are based on preliminary due diligence results and financial analyses.  2. Options, as possibilities, cannot achieve negative values. Their potential implementation is perceived as a benefit or, alternatively, in the event of a negative scenario, the option or pilot project is not implemented. It is therefore assumed that the value of real options does not express the result on the implementation of individual scenarios in the way that managers do. There are no wrong executions of options. Therefore, the value of decision-making flexibility (2MC) will probably be closer to practice, as it also takes into account situations in which a decision made on the basis of positive premises (success of the pilot) may turn out to be wrong (negative value of the extended project). The key assumption concerns knowing the value of the project at the time of option exercise. In practice, this value is subject to stochastic fluctuations, which makes the decision to exercise the option burdened with probabilistic considerations. Managers lack knowledge about the strike price of an option due to the inherent uncertainty in its value.  3.   Conclusion  The results of the valuation indicate a positive value of the real option (EUR 132 million). The valuation was made by comparing two distributions - a project with and without options, which were exposed to the same risks, i.e. price - normal distribution, CAPEX - triangular distribution and the risk of failure of the pilot project. The analysis showed that the model correctly reproduced the option value depending on individual parameters - sensitivity analysis due to the value of investment outlays, the level of volatility, and prices. For comparison, the valuation of decisional flexibility was also performed using the 2MC method - the result was EUR 63 million, with a higher standard deviation. The 2MC method, unlike real option valuation (SCV), allows for negative scenarios in which the option is exercised, resulting in a negative economic result on the project - hence the lower expected value of decision flexibility. If the valuation were to be carried out using alternative methods, e.g. the most popular Black-Scholes method, it would be necessary to analyze many theoretical assumptions, the most important of which are:  •   The need to identify the underlying asset, the necessity to create a hedging portfolio to mitigate option risk; the presence of a single source of uncertainty, the use of geometric Brownian motion with a constant volatility level to describe changes in the value of the underlying asset. All these assumptions force us to make key decisions related to what the underlying asset is, how its values change, whether the risk is to be constant over time and how to reflect it. An attempt to solve this problem may be the MAD approach, which assumes that the twin (base) asset is the value of the project on which the real option is built. It is estimated using discount methods and usually depends on many risky factors.  •   The known and constant strike price of the option - to avoid this issue, decision trees or an appropriate modification of the differential equation should be used. The indicated assumptions indicate that the Black-Scholes model, used to value options that are not standard options of a European nature, will have to be modified. Estimating volatility or the problem of mapping the underlying asset will require the use of market data and many elements of financial modeling. The SCV and 2MC methods, using the well-known framework of discount valuation methods, bypass many of these assumptions, and additionally use directly market data known to managers. The SCV method used in this paper has many advantages. It allows you to answer the question not only about the value of the option, but also to determine the optimal date for its exercise, to assess its financial effects and to assess the sensitivity of the option value to stochastic data and exercise conditions. Financial modeling enables its use in the case of complex, multi-stage options with many sources of risk. A very important feature of this method is the ability to estimate input parameters based on market data, which makes it easier for managers to understand and use the method. Despite its advantages, the SCV method is not without disadvantages, in particular the need to use complex financial modeling and the need to perform numerical operations using specialized software such as Crystal Ball or Pallisade.
